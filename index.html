WHAT IS A PROBLEM-BASED NOTE?

Unlike a single-block SOAP, the problem-based format lists each active issue separately — each with its own mini-assessment and plan. This is how attendings think, how notes are graded, and how you avoid burying the chest pain under the UTI.

Structure: S once (shared) → O once (shared) → A/P per problem, numbered and prioritized by severity.

S — SUBJECTIVE
▸
Patient's story, overnight events, symptoms
WHAT GOES IN SUBJECTIVE

One shared S block at the top — you don't repeat it per problem.

ELEMENTS TO INCLUDE
OVERNIGHT EVENTS
New fevers, desats, rhythm changes, falls, new complaints, nursing concerns. If nothing: "No acute overnight events."
HOW THE PATIENT FEELS TODAY
Quote them directly when helpful: "Patient reports feeling better, pain now 3/10 vs 8/10 yesterday." Always note trajectory — better, worse, or same.
SYMPTOM REVIEW FOR ACTIVE PROBLEMS
Brief symptom check relevant to your problem list — not a full ROS. Chest pain, dyspnea, nausea, bowel/urine output, ambulation.
COMMON INTERN MISTAKE
Writing a full HPI here. The S in a daily progress note is brief — 3–5 lines. Save the full narrative for admissions and H&Ps.
EXAMPLE SUBJECTIVE
55M HD#3 for CAP / sepsis.
No acute overnight events per nursing. Afebrile overnight.
Patient reports improved dyspnea — "breathing is easier." Cough productive, less than yesterday. Appetite returning. No new chest pain or hemoptysis. No diarrhea.
O — OBJECTIVE
▸
Vitals, exam, labs, imaging — what you measured
WHAT GOES IN OBJECTIVE

Also one shared O block. Present it in a logical order: vitals → I/Os → exam → relevant labs → imaging. Don't bury critical values.

VITALS — INCLUDE ALL 24H
Report Tmax (not just current), HR range, BP range, SpO₂ (and O₂ requirement). Always contextualize: Tmax 38.4°C at 02:00, currently 37.1°C.
T: Tmax / current  ·  HR: range  ·  BP: range  ·  RR  ·  SpO₂ on [device/FiO₂]  ·  Weight
I/Os (INPUTS / OUTPUTS)
24h I/O and cumulative balance when relevant (sepsis, CHF, AKI, post-op). I: 1800mL · O: 1200mL · Net: +600mL · Cumulative: +2.4L
PHYSICAL EXAM — PROBLEM-FOCUSED
Lead with general, then systems relevant to your problems. You don't need to document every system every day — focus on what changed and what's pertinent.
Gen: Alert, NAD, sitting up in bed
HEENT: Sclera non-icteric, MM moist
CV: RRR, no murmurs, no JVD, no peripheral edema
Pulm: Decreased BS at R base, dullness to percussion, no wheeze
Abd: Soft, non-tender, non-distended, BS present
Ext: Warm, no calf tenderness, no edema
Neuro: A+Ox3, CN grossly intact
Skin: No new rashes, wound clean/dry/intact
LABS — TREND, DON'T JUST LIST
Report today's value with yesterday's in parentheses. This shows trajectory at a glance. Bold or flag critical values.
WBC 11.2 (↓ from 18.4) · Hgb 9.1 (stable) · Plt 210
Na 138 · K 3.8 · Cl 104 · HCO₃ 24 · BUN 22 · Cr 1.1 (↓ from 1.6)
Lactate 1.2 (↓ from 3.8) · Procalcitonin 1.4 (↓ from 14.2)
Blood cultures: NGTD × 48h
COMMON INTERN MISTAKE
Copy-pasting a wall of labs without interpretation. Your attendings skim — put the signal up front. Abnormals and trends > a full panel.
A/P — ASSESSMENT & PLAN (per problem)
▸
The intellectual core of your note — your thinking
HOW TO STRUCTURE EACH PROBLEM

Number your problems. Put the most acute/life-threatening issue first, not the admitting diagnosis if something more urgent is happening. Each problem block has: name → one-line assessment → plan bullets.

PROBLEM BLOCK ANATOMY
#1. Community-Acquired Pneumonia
— Clinically improving: Tmax trending down, WBC 11 from 18, CXR stable R lower lobe infiltrate. Day 3 of CTX/azithromycin.
  • Continue ceftriaxone 1g IV q24h + azithromycin 500mg PO q24h
  • Target 5-day course — plan PO step-down tomorrow if tolerating
  • Follow up sputum culture — no growth at 48h
  • Maintain O₂ sat >94%, wean supplemental O₂ as tolerated
  • Repeat CXR in 6 weeks to confirm resolution (outpatient)
#2. Sepsis — resolving
— Source = CAP. Lactate normalized (1.2), hemodynamically stable, no pressors. MAP >65 throughout.
  • No further fluid boluses — net positive 2.4L, monitor for fluid overload
  • Blood cultures NGTD — continue to monitor
  • Daily lactate if clinical concern recurs
#3. AKI — resolving
— Cr trending down (1.1 from 1.6 peak). Likely prerenal in setting of sepsis + poor PO. Improving with hydration.
  • Continue IVF at reduced rate — transition to PO intake
  • Trend Cr daily
  • Avoid nephrotoxins — hold NSAIDs, dose-adjust renally cleared meds
  • Reassess need for BMP tomorrow
HOW TO WRITE THE ONE-LINE ASSESSMENT
This is the hardest part for interns. It should answer: "What is going on with this problem and why?" It should NOT just restate what you'll do.
✗ WEAK (no thinking)
CAP — will continue antibiotics.
✓ STRONG (shows synthesis)
CAP — improving clinically and biochemically (WBC ↓, Tmax ↓, lactate normalized). Likely typical bacterial pathogen (rapid antigen neg for flu, minimal atypical risk). On track for 5-day course.
PROBLEM PRIORITIZATION — HOW TO ORDER
1. Most life-threatening active problem first (sepsis, hemodynamic instability, ACS, airway)
2. Admitting diagnosis (if not already #1)
3. Active medical issues (AKI, electrolyte derangements, anemia)
4. Chronic disease management (DM, HTN, COPD — relevant to hospitalization)
5. Prophylaxis & disposition (DVT ppx, foley removal, diet, PT/OT, discharge planning)
STANDING PROBLEMS — ALWAYS ADDRESS
Even if "unchanged," briefly note status so readers know you checked:
#N. DVT prophylaxis — Enoxaparin 40mg SQ qd. No contraindications.
#N. Diet / nutrition — Advanced to regular diet, tolerating POs.
#N. Lines / catheters — PIV only. No Foley. Remove PIV if d/c tomorrow.
#N. Disposition — Target discharge HD#4 if continued improvement. PCP follow-up arranged.
COMMON INTERN MISTAKES
• Listing plans without assessments — every problem needs a one-line "why" before the bullets
• Forgetting discharge planning — start from day 1; attendings will ask every round
• Not addressing chronic meds — held home meds must appear and have a reason
• Missing the safety net — add what you'd watch for: "if worsening X → escalate to Y"
DAILY WORKFLOW — MORNING TO NOTE
▸
How to pre-round, present, and get your notes done
PRE-ROUND SEQUENCE
①
Pull overnight vitals & I/Os
Tmax, HR/BP ranges, O₂ req, fluid balance. Flag anything that changed overnight before you walk in the room.
②
Review morning labs
BMP, CBC, any cultures back, any new results. Note trends vs yesterday — that's the story.
③
Read nursing notes
Nurses document what they see hourly. Overnight events, behavior changes, patient complaints — often the most useful S data.
④
See the patient — exam & brief interview
"How did you sleep? Any new symptoms overnight? Pain now vs yesterday?" Then your exam. Takes 5–8 min. Don't skip it for notes.
⑤
Build your problem list & formulate plans
Before writing a word: decide your problems in priority order and your one-sentence assessment for each. Know your plan before you type it.
⑥
Write the note
S → O → A/P per problem. Target: note done before rounds so your attending can read it. A note on rounds is better than no note, but barely.
PRESENTATION FORMAT (1-LINER THEN SYSTEMS)
Opening sentence: [Name], [age][sex], HD#[X] for [primary diagnosis], now presenting with [major overnight development or "no acute overnight events"].
"Mr. Smith is a 55-year-old man, hospital day 3 for community-acquired pneumonia with sepsis, who has no acute overnight events and reports improvement in dyspnea."
Then: vitals → I/Os → exam findings → labs → plan per problem. Keep it to 3–4 minutes per patient or you will lose your team.
FULL EXAMPLE NOTE — CAP / SEPSIS / AKI
▸
Complete annotated progress note you can model
INTERNAL MEDICINE PROGRESS NOTE — HD#3
SUBJECTIVE
55M HD#3 for CAP with sepsis and AKI.
No acute overnight events per nursing. Afebrile since 0200.
Patient reports significant improvement in breathing — "I feel much better." Cough productive with decreased frequency. Tolerating oral fluids. No new chest pain, hemoptysis, or diarrhea. Ambulated to bathroom independently this morning.

OBJECTIVE
Vitals (24h): Tmax 38.4°C (0200) → 37.1°C now | HR 78–102 | BP 112–134/70–84 | RR 18 | SpO₂ 95% on 2L NC (was 4L) | Wt 82kg
I/O (24h): In 2100mL / Out 1750mL / Net +350mL / Cumulative +2.8L

Exam:
Gen: Alert, NAD, conversational, sitting up eating breakfast
HEENT: MM moist, no LAD
CV: RRR, no murmurs/rubs/gallops, no JVD
Pulm: Dullness to percussion at R base, decreased BS R>L, no wheeze, improved from yesterday
Abd: Soft, non-tender, non-distended, normoactive BS
Ext: Warm, well-perfused, trace bilateral ankle edema (new — likely resuscitation-related), no calf tenderness
Neuro: A+Ox3, appropriate

Labs:
WBC 11.2 (↓18.4) | Hgb 9.8 (stable) | Plt 210
Na 138 | K 4.1 | Cr 1.1 (↓1.6 peak) | BUN 24 | HCO₃ 23
Lactate 1.2 (↓3.8) | Procalcitonin 1.4 (↓14.2)
BCx ×2: NGTD at 60h | Sputum: GPC in pairs, moderate PMNs, final pending

Imaging: CXR (AM): R lower lobe consolidation, stable vs yesterday. No new effusion.

ASSESSMENT & PLAN
#1. Community-Acquired Pneumonia — Clinically improving
Tmax trending down, WBC halved, O₂ requirement reduced from 4L→2L NC. Sputum Gram stain with GPC — S. pneumoniae most likely. On track for 5-day course.
  • Continue ceftriaxone 1g IV q24h; plan PO amoxicillin 1g TID step-down tomorrow if tolerating
  • Continue azithromycin 500mg PO q24h (day 3 of 5)
  • Titrate O₂ to SpO₂ >94%; wean to RA as tolerated
  • Await final sputum cultures — narrow if sensitivities available
  • Repeat CXR at discharge; outpatient follow-up CXR in 6 weeks

#2. Sepsis — Resolved
Source = CAP. Lactate normal, hemodynamically stable ×24h, no vasopressors. Fluid resuscitation complete.
  • Antibiotics as above
  • Hold further IVF — cumulative positive 2.8L, note trace edema
  • Blood cultures NGTD — no source control issue

#3. AKI (Cr 1.6 → 1.1) — Resolving
Prerenal etiology in setting of sepsis-related hypoperfusion and poor PO intake. Responding to IV hydration. No contrast, no nephrotoxin exposure.
  • Advance diet — encourage oral fluids; d/c maintenance IVF
  • Trend Cr daily; target BMP tomorrow
  • Hold NSAIDs indefinitely; renally dose any new meds
  • If Cr not trending to baseline by discharge → outpatient nephrology referral

#4. New trace bilateral ankle edema
Likely resuscitation-related fluid shift rather than cardiac decompensation — no JVD, no new dyspnea, HR controlled. Low suspicion for new CHF in this context.
  • No diuresis at this time — will reassess in 24h
  • If respiratory status worsens or edema increases → repeat BNP, consider echo

#5. DM Type 2 (home: metformin)
Metformin held on admission (AKI + contrast risk). Fingersticks 120–180 range on hospital insulin protocol.
  • Continue sliding scale insulin
  • Resume metformin at discharge once Cr at baseline

#6. Prophylaxis / Safety
  • DVT ppx: Enoxaparin 40mg SQ qd — no contraindications
  • GI ppx: Not indicated (no high-risk features, not on steroids)
  • Lines: PIV only. No Foley. No central line.
  • Diet: Regular diet; advance as tolerated
  • Activity: Ambulate TID with nursing

#7. Disposition
Target discharge HD#4 pending: continued clinical improvement, Cr trending to baseline, O₂ wean to RA, tolerating PO antibiotics. PCP follow-up within 1 week. Discharge CXR planned.
PEARLS & PITFALLS
▸
What separates a good intern note from a bad one
✦ THINK IN PROBLEMS, NOT SYSTEMS
A system-based note (CV, Pulm, Renal…) forces the reader to mentally regroup. A problem-based note mirrors how consultants and attendings think — and it makes your reasoning visible.
✦ YOUR ASSESSMENT IS YOUR MOST IMPORTANT SENTENCE
Anyone can copy-paste labs. The one-line synthesis ("improving/worsening/stable because X") is what demonstrates you understand the patient. This is what attendings evaluate.
✦ TREND EVERYTHING
Cr 1.1 means nothing. Cr 1.1 (↓ from 1.6 yesterday, peak 2.2 on admission) tells the whole story. Always show direction and time context.
✦ HELD HOME MEDS MUST APPEAR
Every held home medication needs to show up on your problem list — with a reason (AKI, NPO, renal dosing) and a plan to restart. Missing this is a patient safety issue and a billing issue.
✦ DISCHARGE PLANNING STARTS ON DAY 1
What criteria need to be met for discharge? What follow-up does the patient need? Who needs to be notified (PCP, subspecialty)? Write this on every note — even day 1. It forces you to define your endpoint.
✦ ADDRESS THE INCIDENTAL FINDINGS
Lung nodule on CT done for another reason. Mildly elevated ALT. Incidentaloma. These must appear in your note — even if just "incidentally noted, will follow up outpatient." Ignoring them creates medicolegal exposure.
✦ NEVER SIGN A NOTE YOU HAVEN'T ACTED ON
If you write "check BMP tomorrow" — order it. If you write "discuss with cardiology" — call them. Your note is a contract. Your attending will verify your actions match your note on rounds.
✦ THE "IF/THEN" RULE
For any unstable problem, include what you're watching for and what you'll do: "If respiratory status worsens or O₂ req increases → CXR, consider HFNC, pulm consult." This shows anticipatory thinking — attendings love it.
